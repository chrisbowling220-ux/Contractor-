# How to Pitch BuildPro+ — How to Talk to Buyers

This is your **guide for talking to people** — in person or on the phone. It's
not about what the app does (that's in the presentation). It's about **how to
say it** so you come across sure of yourself, sharp, and worth listening to.

The big secret first: **sounding smart isn't about big words. It's about being
calm, clear, and sure of yourself.** The people who impress a room are the ones
who say simple things slowly and don't get rattled. You already know this app
better than anyone alive. Act like it.

---

## The 3 Rules That Make You Sound Like a Pro

### Rule 1 — Talk about THEM, not the app.
Beginners list features. Pros talk about how the buyer's life gets better.

- ❌ "It's got an AI material list maker with ZIP-code pricing."
- ✅ "You take a photo, talk for 20 seconds, and the quote's done — priced for
  your area. The thing that used to eat your whole evening takes you 30 seconds
  on the truck."

Every sentence should answer one question from the contractor's side: *"What's
in it for me?"* When you're not sure, end your sentence with **"…so you get home
for dinner"** or **"…so you stop losing money on it."**

### Rule 2 — Slow down and use fewer words.
Nervous people talk fast and explain too much. Sure people pause. A 2-second
quiet moment after a strong line makes you look *more* sure, not less. Make your
point, then stop talking. Let it sink in. **The person who's okay with silence
runs the room.**

### Rule 3 — Never make stuff up. "Good question — let me get you that."
You're going to get asked something you don't know. That's normal, and it is NOT
a fail. The dumb move is making something up. The smart move:

> "Good question. I don't want to give you half an answer — let me get you the
> exact number and call you back today."

That one habit makes you look *more* honest than someone who has a slick answer
for everything. Being straight with people comes across as confidence.

## Your 60-Second Pitch (learn the shape, not the exact words)

Practice this till it feels natural. It's four steps:

**1. The hook (the problem they feel):**
> "You know how after a full day on the job, you still gotta go home and write
> quotes at the kitchen table for two hours? That's the part I killed."

**2. What it is (one sentence):**
> "BuildPro+ is an app that does all the office work — quotes, invoices, getting
> paid — right from your phone, while you're still on the job."

**3. The proof (don't tell them — show them; pull out your phone):**
> "Here, watch. I take a photo of this room… I talk through the job…" *(do it
> right there)* "…and there's the full material list, priced for the ZIP, with a
> total. I just text that to the customer and they sign it on their phone."

**4. The close (give them the next step):**
> "It's free to try — five quotes, no card. Want me to set you up right now so
> you can run your next bid through it?"

That's it. Hook → what it is → proof → ask. Under a minute.

## The Live Demo IS Your Pitch — Do It Every Time

Nothing you *say* beats them *seeing* a quote build in 30 seconds. Always have:

- The app open and **logged in** before you walk up (never fumble the login).
- **A real photo ready to go** — a bathroom or a deck works great.
- A 15-second job description you can say smoothly:
  *"Gut this bathroom — new tile floor, tile shower walls, new vanity, toilet,
  and paint all around."*
- Wi-Fi or good signal. Test it before you're standing in front of someone.

**Run the demo 20 times by yourself first.** The goal is you could do it half
asleep. When it's smooth, it looks like magic. When you fumble, it looks shaky.

> Pro move: after the material list pops up, go quiet for a second, then say one
> line: *"That just took 30 seconds. By hand that's an hour at your kitchen
> table."* Let them do the math in their head. Don't oversell it.

## How to Handle the Hard Questions (without sweating)

Buyers will test you. Here's how a pro answers the common ones. Notice the
pattern: **agree → answer plain → bring it back to what they get.**

**"Is this just ChatGPT?" / "It's all that AI hype, right?"**
> "What's under the hood doesn't matter to you — what matters is it builds an
> accurate quote in 30 seconds and you can fix any line with one tap before it
> goes out. You're always in charge of the number."

**"What if it gets the materials wrong?"**
> "Fair — that's why every line can be changed. It gets you 90% there in
> seconds, and you fix the last 10% if you want. It's a head start, not your
> boss. Beats a blank notepad at 9 at night."

**"How's my payment and customer info kept safe?"**
> "Payments run through Stripe — the same company that handles cards for
> millions of businesses; card numbers never touch the app. And every
> contractor's stuff is walled off to their own account. Nobody sees anybody
> else's jobs."

**"Why would I pay 20 bucks a month?"**
> "If it saves you two hours a week — and it saves a lot more — that's eight
> hours a month. What's eight hours of your time worth? Twenty bucks isn't even
> close." *(Then stop talking.)*

**"I've gotten by fine without it for 20 years."**
> "No doubt — you clearly know your trade. This isn't about the work, it's about
> the paperwork after. Run one bid through it free and see if it saves you a
> night. If it doesn't, you've lost nothing."

**"Does it do [some specific trade thing]?"** (and it might not yet)
> "Let me show you what it does today, and if that one thing matters to you, tell
> me — I'm building this around what real contractors need." *(This turns a gap
> into a relationship. Write down what they ask for.)*

## Little Things That Make You Sound Sharp

**Do:**
- **Talk in plain, sure sentences.** "It builds the quote for you." Done.
- **Use their words:** bid, job, scope, materials, change order, getting paid.
  Not "user," "workflow," "platform," "solution." Trade words = you're one of
  them. Fancy words = you sound like a salesman.
- **Tell them why you built it** — it's your edge: *"I built this because I was
  sick of doing it by hand."* People buy from someone who gets their problem.
- **Ask questions and listen.** "How do you do your quotes now?" Let them gripe
  about the kitchen table — then show them the fix. A pitch is a back-and-forth,
  not a speech.
- **Use a real number.** "30 seconds." "Two hours a night." "Five free quotes."
  Real numbers sound true; vague sounds made up.

**Don't:**
- Don't say "um," "like," "I think," "kinda," "maybe," "sorta." Swap them for a
  **pause.** Quiet beats filler, every time.
- Don't apologize for it or talk it down ("it's not perfect but…"). Say what it
  does and let them judge. Being sure is part of the sell.
- Don't dump every feature at once. Show the quote demo, then **let them ask**
  for more. Curiosity sells; drowning them in features doesn't.
- Don't argue if they're unsure. "Totally fair — try it free and decide for
  yourself." You never lose by staying calm.
- Don't talk money until they're interested. Show the value first, price second,
  always.

## Before You Walk In — 90-Second Confidence Check

1. **Know your one line cold:** *"It does the office side of contracting from
   your phone so you stop losing your nights."* If you can only say that, you've
   already won.
2. **Phone charged, app open, demo photo loaded, signal tested.**
3. **Stand up straight, slow your breathing, talk a little slower and a little
   lower than feels natural.** That's what "sure of yourself" looks like from the
   outside.
4. **Flip the nerves around:** you're not begging them to buy. You built
   something genuinely useful and you're *offering* it. You're doing them a
   favor. Walk in with that.
5. **The goal of the talk isn't "sell" — it's "get them to try it free."** That's
   a tiny ask. Aim for the free signup, and the app sells itself.

---

## The Two Lines to End On

If you forget everything else, end every pitch with these:

> **"It's free to try — five quotes, no card. Can I set you up right now?"**

Getting the app in their hands today beats the best speech you could give. The
demo wins them; the free try closes them; the time it saves keeps them.

You built this. Nobody can pitch it better than the person who made it because
they were sick of the problem. Go be that person.
